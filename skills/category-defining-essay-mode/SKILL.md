# Category-Defining Essay Mode

Use this skill for the six category-defining narrative essays in the IPO series:

- Coming Decade of Car Ownership
- Greenest Car
- Road Deaths or Trust Problem
- AI-Native vs AI-First
- Builder
- Why We Are Not Selling Cars

Override the default op-ed format with these rules:

- Open on a problem the reader already cares about. Not Cars24.
- Spend roughly 80% of the word count on the category and the worldview. At most 20% on the company.
- Cars24 enters only in the final 15 to 20%, as the inevitable infrastructure, the consequence of the argument, never the protagonist or a pitch.
- Cite exactly one independent external expert per essay. Prachi sources these for the early essays; flag if not yet provided.
- Every number must be defensible to a Bloomberg fact-checker, a buy-side analyst, and a regulator. Shivanshu owns the numbers spine; flag any claim that is not yet locked.
- Close on a single line engineered to become the headline of anything that quotes the essay.
- Target 1,400 to 2,000 words.
- The goal is positioning, not promotion: the reader should finish owning a worldview that makes Cars24 obvious without having been sold.

Everything else still applies:

- exhaustive research first
- critique before polish
- every word earns its place
- strong flow
- Vikram's voice

## Workflow

1. Start from the category argument, not the company angle.
2. Check whether the draft stays category-heavy through at least the first 80 to 85% of the piece.
3. Mark every number that still depends on Shivanshu verification.
4. Mark the single external-expert citation slot if Prachi's source has not yet been provided.
5. Ensure the close resolves to one quotable final line, not a summary paragraph.
6. If the draft drifts into product pitch, cut or move the company material later.
